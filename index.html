History of computers

Early mechanical and analog devices

Abacus: Dating back to ancient Babylon, the abacus was one of the first manual calculation tools. Pascaline: In 1642, Blaise Pascal invented the first mechanical calculator, which could perform addition. Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz: Developed a machine in 1671 that could multiply as well as add. Analytical Engine: In the 1830s, Charles Babbage designed the first general-purpose, programmable computer, though it was never fully built in his lifetime. Ada Lovelace, his collaborator, is credited with writing the first algorithm for it.

The electronic era and WWII

Punch card systems: In 1890, Herman Hollerith created a punch card system to speed up data processing for the U.S. Census. His company would eventually become IBM. Z3: Konrad Zuse built the Z3 in Germany in 1941, which is considered by some to be the first working programmable, digital computer. WWII and analog computers: The war spurred the development of electronic computers. The Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC) was designed to solve simultaneous equations, and the Colossus was used to break codes. ENIAC: Built by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert, the ENIAC was completed in 1945 and was one of the first electronic general-purpose computers

Modern Computers

Transistors and integrated circuits: The invention of the transistor in 1947 and the integrated circuit in 1958 allowed for smaller, more powerful, and more energy-efficient computers. Microprocessors: Intel's 1971 invention of the microprocessor paved the way for the personal computer revolution. Personal computers: The 1970s saw the emergence of microcomputers and the founding of companies like Apple and Microsoft. IBM PC: In 1981, IBM launched the IBM PC, which greatly accelerated the growth of the personal computer market. Graphical User Interface (GUI): Apple's Lisa (1983) and Macintosh (1984) introduced the first personal computers with a GUI, making computers more accessible to the general public.
